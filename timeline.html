1800s

Residential Schools Begin

The Canadian government and churches establish residential schools. Indigenous children are forcibly removed from their families.

Speaking Indigenous languages is forbidden and often punished. This marks the beginning of large-scale language suppression.

👉 Impact: Many children lose fluency and cannot pass the language to the next generation.

1900s

Language Suppression Continues

For over a century, Indigenous languages are discouraged in schools, homes, and public life.

Many communities experience “language break” between generations. Elders remain fluent, but younger generations are often not.

👉 Impact: Several Indigenous languages become endangered or severely reduced in speakers.

2008

Government Apology

The Government of Canada formally apologizes for the residential school system.

This is a symbolic step acknowledging the harm caused to Indigenous communities, including cultural and linguistic loss.

👉 Impact: Increased public awareness and beginning of reconciliation efforts.

2015

Truth and Reconciliation Commission

The TRC releases 94 Calls to Action, highlighting the need to address the legacy of residential schools.

Call to Action #14 specifically calls for the protection, revitalization, and strengthening of Indigenous languages.

👉 Impact: Language preservation becomes a national responsibility.

2019

Indigenous Languages Act

Canada passes the Indigenous Languages Act. It recognizes Indigenous languages as part of Canadian culture and identity.

The law supports funding, education programs, and community-led revitalization.

👉 Impact: Formal government support for language revival efforts.

Today

Language Revitalization Today

Indigenous communities are actively rebuilding languages through schools, cultural programs, and digital media.

Social media creators teach words, phrases, and pronunciation to younger generations.

👉 Impact: Languages are being revived and normalized in daily life.
